General Questions IRDAI

Health insurance is a contract between you and an insurance company where the insurer agrees to cover your medical expenses in exchange for a premium. It provides financial protection against hospitalization, surgeries, doctor consultations, and other healthcare costs. In India, health insurance plans are regulated by the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI).

Health insurance is not mandatory for all citizens in India. However, it is highly recommended due to rising healthcare costs. Certain categories like central government employees (covered under CGHS) and some state government employees have mandatory coverage. The government provides free health coverage under Ayushman Bharat for eligible economically vulnerable families.

An individual plan covers one person with a specific sum insured. A family floater plan covers the entire family under a single sum insured that is shared among all members. Family floater plans are generally more cost-effective for young families, while individual plans may be better when covering elderly parents due to higher risk.

Yes, you can have multiple health insurance policies. In case of a claim, you can file with one insurer and then claim the remaining amount from the other under the contribution clause or top-up provisions. However, you cannot make a profit from insurance; the total claim amount cannot exceed the actual medical expenses incurred.

Coverage & Benefits IRDAI

Standard health insurance plans in India typically cover:

  • Hospitalization expenses (room rent, ICU, surgeon fees, medicines)
  • Pre-hospitalization expenses (usually 30-60 days before)
  • Post-hospitalization expenses (usually 60-90 days after)
  • Ambulance charges
  • Daycare procedures (surgeries not requiring 24-hour hospitalization)
  • Preventive health checkups
  • Cashless treatment at network hospitals

Common exclusions include:

  • Pre-existing diseases (covered after waiting period of 2-4 years)
  • Cosmetic surgery (unless medically necessary)
  • Dental treatment (unless accidental)
  • War, nuclear risks, and self-inflicted injuries
  • Alcohol and drug-related treatment
  • Maternity expenses (covered in specific plans with waiting period)
  • Alternative treatments (AYUSH may be covered in some plans)

A waiting period is the time you must wait after purchasing a policy before certain benefits become available. In India:

  • Initial waiting period: Usually 30 days (not applicable for accidents)
  • Pre-existing disease waiting period: Typically 2-4 years
  • Specific disease waiting period: 1-2 years for conditions like hernia, cataract, etc.
  • Maternity waiting period: Usually 9 months to 2 years

Copayment is a clause where you agree to share a percentage of the claim amount with the insurer. For example, a 20% copayment means you pay 20% of the claim, and the insurer pays 80%. Policies with copayment usually have lower premiums. Some policies mandate copayment for senior citizens or specific treatments.

Claims Process IRDAI

Cashless claim: When you get treatment at a network hospital of your insurer, the hospital bills the insurance company directly. You do not need to pay upfront (subject to policy coverage limits).

Reimbursement claim: You pay the hospital bills yourself and then submit the documents to your insurance company for reimbursement. This applies when you use a non-network hospital or in emergency situations.

For cashless claims, inform the insurer within 24-48 hours of admission. The hospital's insurance desk will guide you through the process. You need to present your health insurance card and ID proof.

For reimbursement claims, collect all documents (discharge summary, bills, prescriptions, investigation reports) and submit them to your insurer within the specified timeline (usually 7-15 days from discharge). Many insurers now allow online claim submission.

Typical documents required:

  • Duly filled claim form
  • Original hospital bills and receipts
  • Discharge summary from the hospital
  • Doctor's prescriptions and medical reports
  • Investigation reports (X-ray, lab tests, etc.)
  • Pharmacy bills for medicines
  • Valid ID proof and health insurance card
  • Bank account details for reimbursement

The claim settlement ratio (CSR) is the percentage of claims an insurer has settled compared to the total claims received. It is an important indicator of an insurer's reliability. IRDAI publishes these ratios annually. A higher CSR (above 90%) generally indicates better claim settlement performance.

Premiums & Costs IRDAI

Key factors that affect your premium:

  • Age: Premiums increase as you get older
  • Sum insured: Higher coverage means higher premium
  • Medical history: Pre-existing conditions may increase premium
  • Lifestyle: Smoking, alcohol consumption, and BMI
  • Plan type: Individual vs family floater, with or without add-ons
  • Network hospitals: Plans with wider network may cost more
  • Location: Premiums vary by city due to healthcare costs

Yes, under Section 80D of the Income Tax Act, you can claim deductions on health insurance premiums paid:

  • Up to Rs. 25,000 for yourself, spouse, and children
  • Up to Rs. 50,000 if you or your spouse is a senior citizen
  • Up to Rs. 25,000 for parents (Rs. 50,000 if parents are senior citizens)
  • Up to Rs. 5,000 for preventive health checkups (within overall limit)

Ways to reduce premium without compromising coverage:

  • Choose a higher deductible (voluntary excess)
  • Opt for a family floater plan instead of individual plans
  • Maintain a healthy lifestyle (no-claim bonus, lower BMI)
  • Compare plans from different insurers
  • Buy early when you are younger
  • Consider a top-up plan for additional coverage
  • Look for discounts on multi-year policies

Government Schemes Govt. of India

Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana (PM-JAY) is the world's largest government-funded health insurance scheme. It provides coverage of up to Rs. 5 lakh per family per year for secondary and tertiary care hospitalization. The scheme targets economically vulnerable families based on SECC data. It covers over 10 crore families and offers cashless treatment at empaneled hospitals across India.

The Central Government Health Scheme (CGHS) provides comprehensive healthcare coverage to central government employees, pensioners, and their dependents. It offers outpatient and inpatient treatment, medicines, diagnostics, and preventive care through a network of CGHS wellness centers and empaneled hospitals. Membership is available to eligible individuals in designated cities.

The Employees' State Insurance Corporation (ESIC) provides health insurance to employees earning up to Rs. 21,000 per month (Rs. 25,000 for persons with disability). It covers medical treatment, hospitalization, maternity benefits, and disability benefits. Both employees and employers contribute to the scheme. ESIC has its own network of hospitals and dispensaries.

Yes, many states in India have their own health insurance schemes. Examples include:

  • Maharashtra: Mahatma Jyotiba Phule Jan Arogya Yojana
  • Karnataka: Yeshasvini Health Insurance Scheme
  • Andhra Pradesh: YSR Aarogyasri Health Scheme
  • Tamil Nadu: Chief Minister's Comprehensive Health Insurance Scheme
  • Gujarat: Mukhyamantri Amrutam Yojana

These schemes typically provide coverage to residents below the poverty line or specific beneficiary groups.
